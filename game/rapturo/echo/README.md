Echoes of the Unbuilt — Full Game Explanation
1. What the Game Is

Echoes of the Unbuilt is a single-player, first-person systemic sandbox where the world is incomplete by design.

The world does not have fixed laws.
Instead, it runs on mutable rules that the player can observe, modify, stabilize, or erase.

You are not a hero, soldier, or survivor.

You are The Architect — an intelligence created to finalize a world that was abandoned mid-development.

2. The Player’s Purpose (This Is Crucial)

There is no traditional objective like:

Kill enemies

Reach an end boss

Save the world

Your purpose is:

To decide what kind of reality deserves to exist.

The game ends when:

The world becomes stable enough to run without you

OR unstable enough to collapse

OR self-aware enough to reject you

Your choices define which ending occurs.

3. How the World Actually Works
3.1 The World Is a System, Not a Map

The world is made of regions.
Each region has:

Its own rule set

Its own entropy level

NPCs with local beliefs

Example:

One region has gravity but no collision

Another has collision but frozen time

A third contradicts both

When regions interact:
➡️ Rules conflict
➡️ Bugs appear
➡️ New behaviors emerge

This is intentional.

4. The Rule System (Core Mechanic)
4.1 What a “Rule” Is

A rule is a fundamental law of reality.

Examples:

Gravity exists

Objects collide

Time flows forward

Memory persists

Death is permanent

Rules are modular and toggleable.

They are not settings — they are gameplay choices.

4.2 How You Interact With Rules

You do not open a god menu.

Instead, you:

Discover rules by observing glitches

Extract rules from broken objects

Inherit rules from NPC beliefs

Stabilize or suppress them at “Anchor Points”

Each change:

Fixes something

Breaks something else

There is no safe choice.

5. Entropy — The World’s Health

Entropy measures how contradictory reality has become.

Low Entropy

Predictable behavior

Stable NPCs

Clear cause and effect

High Entropy

Objects change state mid-interaction

NPCs remember things that never happened

Geometry rearranges itself

The UI itself becomes unreliable

Entropy is not “bad”.
High entropy unlocks impossible structures and emergent behaviors.

But too much:
➡️ The world stops trusting you.

6. NPCs — Not Characters, Belief Systems

NPCs do not follow scripts.

Each NPC has:

Needs (safety, movement, meaning)

Beliefs (weighted probabilities)

Memory (what they think happened)

Example beliefs:

“Walls protect me” (0.9)

“The Architect watches” (0.3)

“Reality resets at dusk” (0.6)

6.1 How NPCs React to You

NPCs observe:

Rule changes

World inconsistencies

Your proximity

If you:

Break gravity near them → they lose trust in physics

Delete collision → they stop hiding

Reset time → some remember, some don’t

NPCs adapt, panic, worship, or resist.

No dialogue trees needed.

7. Progression (Without Levels)

You don’t gain XP.

You gain:

Access to deeper rules

Responsibility over larger regions

Consequences that propagate further

Progression is cognitive, not numeric.

You understand the system better — and the world reacts accordingly.

8. Failure and Death

You cannot “lose” traditionally.

If the world:

Fully stabilizes → it no longer needs you (Ending)

Fully destabilizes → it collapses (Ending)

Becomes self-aware → it traps or erases you (Ending)

Your “death” may:

Reset you

Rewrite you

Turn you into an NPC

Depending on entropy and beliefs.

9. The Endings (Defined by Your Behavior)

Examples:

Ending 1 — The Perfect Fragment

You stabilize a small region perfectly.
The rest of the world is deleted.
You leave behind a flawless, tiny reality.

Ending 2 — The Infinite Error

You embrace entropy.
Reality becomes vast, beautiful, and broken.
The world runs forever — incorrectly.

Ending 3 — The World Without an Architect

NPCs rewrite the rules themselves.
You are no longer allowed to interfere.

Ending 4 — Assimilation

You become part of the simulation.
No longer external. No longer in control.
